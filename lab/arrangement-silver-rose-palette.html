A silver ground, a graphic dark line, one rare living color — and, confirmed by radial measurement, an amber corona at the center of the eye. Every hex below was measured from the original eleven photographs or derived by stated arithmetic from a measured hue. Tap any drape to copy it.

I · The Sitter, Measured what you are made of — including the corona, found

The eye is a two-country landscape, measured: hue rotates from 53° at the pupil to 94° at the rim — an amber-olive inner zone resolving into the gray-green field. The first analysis tested yellowness alone and missed warmth carried in redness; the radial test found it. You said it existed. It does.

II · The Painting Field the original question — seventeen paintings, two calibrations, three data treatments

You live on an axis between two worlds. One pole is the Tonalist silver atmosphere — Morisot, Monet, Lady Agnew — where your surfaces are native. The other is graphic pallor-on-dark — Madame X, Vermeer — where your structure is native. Your iris is your universal passport (top-three in every leading painting; ΔE 2.4 inside Madame X). Your hair is what Morisot claims. Your flush is your rarest color — the one old-master palettes barely stock — the pigment you must supply yourself. The stable bottom: saturated warmth and dark interiors (Botticelli 13th–14th, Ophelia 15th–16th, Hammershøi last). Containment measures harmony, not ignition — the mirror still outranks the math.

IV · The Wardrobe — The Half-Lights the register between veil and trap, L≈73–78 — lawful: it belongs to the light pole

Why this shelf is legal: the trap's core is foreign mutes at L≈48–68. Above that band, soft color reads as an extension of your luminous surface — a light, slightly weathered. Wear these as grounds with one true pole nearby, and they behave.

V · The Wardrobe — The Second Skins your own midtones — the lawful exceptions

Why these midtones are exempt from the midtone law: they are you. A mid-value mute that matches your own hair or iris reads as an extension of your body — a neutral, like skin in fabric form. A foreign mid-value mute reads as fog. Same register, opposite verdict: the difference is whether the color already lives on your head.

VI · The Wardrobe — The Bridges midtones, lawfully admitted — each carries its visa

Three visas admit a midtone: kinship (it is one of your own hues, worn at mid-value), chroma (lifted to C≈18–26 it reads as color instead of fog — the whole difference between the slate that failed you at C≈10 and a French blue at C22), or pole-lean. The wearing rule is unchanged: a bridge is pinned to at least one worn pole — it carries the outfit across the gulf; it never is the outfit.

VIII · The Rule of the Middle foreign midtones, illustrated — demoted, not forbidden

Mid-value muted color holding no visa — not your hue, not chroma-lifted, not pole-leaning — lives between your poles and belongs to neither — the measured mechanism of every mousy failure: slate, plum smoke, damson, bordeaux-smoke, mid-olive. A foreign midtone earns its place only as a bridge pinned between two worn poles, never as the outfit's voice.

X · The Metals settled by measurement — your hair is chroma-4 silver

Sterling leads; gold whispers — now with one earned exception: the corona gives muted antique gold a small legitimate harmony at the face (it repeats the eye's amber center). Sterling, pewter, and white gold still lead — they repeat the larger fact. Stones: moonstone, gray pearl, labradorite, rose quartz; amber or golden citrine small, as corona-kin; rose garnet or dark sapphire for the pole.

XII · The Silhouette committed calls from your visible architecture — long vertical, soft curve, one graphic line

The Essence Blend

Romantic 35 · Dramatic 25 · Ethereal 20 · Classic 15 · Natural 5. Softness with substance leads; the long decisive line is non-negotiable; the light is load-bearing. Every garment call below serves those three masters at once.

The Body Line

Keep the vertical unbroken: columns, bias slips, full-length trousers, long open layers. The waist is the hinge — define it (wrap, belt, seam). Shoulders are narrow and soft: shaped seams yes, boxy volume and dropped shoulders no — they erase your line.

Fit Doctrine

Fitted or draped — never boxy. The bodice acknowledges the curve; volume, when worn, gathers back into a line (a cuffed bishop sleeve, a belted trench, a slip that pools). Chunky oversized knits swallow you; fine fitted knits crown you.

XIII · The Garment Map piece by piece — what serves the arrangement

Necklines

V, wrap, surplice, scoop, portrait, off-shoulder, soft cowl. The ink or black turtleneck is Madame X as knitwear — yours. Skip: crew necks in foreign midtones, fussy high ruffles that argue with the brow.

Sleeves & Shoulders

Long fitted, bracelet, soft bishop gathered to a cuff. A clean set-in shoulder or a whisper of structure. Skip: aggressive puffs, dropped seams, cap sleeves that stop the eye at your narrowest point.

Skirts & Dresses

Bias slip, column, wrap, sheath, long A-line with movement. Hems long or true-mini — the awkward mid-calf-stiff zone chops the vertical the way midtones chop the palette.

Trousers & Denim

High-rise wide-leg, straight, trouser-cut jeans in Mid Denim, Indigo, ink, or oyster. Full length over ankle-crop. The denim shelf exists so your casual body keeps its column.

Outerwear

The long-line coat is your power object: ink navy, espresso, oyster, sterling gray. A belted trench in Silver White or ink. Leather in Espresso Ink or black — never warm tan.

Fabric Shelf

Silk crepe, washed satin, velvet in pole colors, fine merino, cashmere, suede, brushed wool, crisp poplin for the white shirt. Matte-luminous always; loud gloss and stiff shine out-chroma you.

XIV · The Finishing shoes, bags, eyewear, nails, hair — the last ten percent that reads first

Shoes

Pointed and almond profiles, sleek boots in ink or espresso, your nude is Shell-to-Rose-Taupe, metallic flats in sterling. Sleek over chunky: the shoe finishes the column or breaks it.

Bags

Structured, medium scale, silver hardware. Oyster, ink navy, espresso, pewter leather. One sculptural object, not an accumulation — same rule as the jewelry.

Eyewear

Elongated soft rectangles and gentle cat-eyes — the frame repeats your vertical and lifts with the brow, never covers it. Pewter, ink navy, translucent rose-gray; tortoise only in cool ash-brown, never honey.

Nails

Sheer Rose Veil or Shell for every day; Rose Garnet or Ink Navy as the statement. Short-to-medium, polished shape — the hand echoes the palette, quietly.

Hair, Styled

Length with movement: long layers, polished wave, face-framing, the low knot that shows the neck. Depth kept at the root, ribbons only in ash and pearl — and the sterling displayed at the face like the jewelry it is.

Jewelry, Restated

Threaders, drops, lariats — long lines in sterling and pewter. Moonstone as your iris, gray pearl as your surface, amber small as the corona, rose garnet or dark sapphire as the pole. One sculptural cuff beats a wrist of clutter.

XVI · The Laws the whole system, compressed — full portrait in the document

Law One — Low chroma

Nothing you own exceeds C≈21. High saturation performs beside you; it never joins you. Surfaces matte-luminous: crepe, velvet, fine merino, washed satin.

Law Two — Poles, not midtones

L85 surface, L45 anchors, 40 units of gulf. Wear the poles straight — black, silver-white, ink navy included. Foreign midtones are the mousy trap; your own midtones are second skins.

Law Three — The rose is the rarity

Your flush is the color painting undersupplies. Rose is not decoration; it is the missing pigment of your own portrait — supply it at the lip, the collar, the cheek.

Law Four — The closet knew

Navy is the darkened complement of your own eyes — both zones of them. Black and white are your measured poles. Black-white-navy was never a retreat; it was the derivation, worn early.

The One Formula

A pole at the face, a pole at the anchor, one live wire. Necklines are contrast placement: V and wrap for the vertical; an ink turtleneck is Madame X as knitwear. Prints must carry a pole.

The Signature

Head-to-toe ink navy, sterling at the ear, nothing else. The quietest sentence that says everything the measurements say.
